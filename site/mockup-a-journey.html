Maria asked an AI
about her tiny house.
Six months later—

three versions of the same chatbot gave her three different answers. One cost her $4,200 and a demolished foundation. One stalled her for eight weeks. One got her permitted by Memorial Day. This is what the difference between “an AI” and “an AI that can actually look things up” looks like in a real life.

Maria works as a pediatric dental hygienist at OHSU. Her mom, Lupe, is moving up from Reno next spring — the long-distance care thing has finally tipped — and Maria wants her on the property but in her own space. Privacy for both of them. A real door.

She has roughly $95,000 saved for construction, a contractor cousin in Beaverton, and a 4,800 sq ft lot in Sunnyside with a big pear tree out back. She has zero experience with city permitting. So in November, on a slow Sunday, she opens her laptop and does what anyone would do.

Day 0 · Nov 12, 2025 Sunday, 9:47 PM · Maria sends one question
The AI sounds certain. Maria takes notes.
ChatGPT · reply Building a tiny house in Portland is generally allowed under the city's Accessory Dwelling Unit (ADU) rules. Most residential lots permit a second small dwelling of up to 1,000 square feet. Setbacks are typically 5 feet from all property lines. You'll likely need a building permit—contact Portland's permit office.
“Portland zoning code” no section #

Maria writes down 1,000 sq ft, 5-foot setbacks. She texts her cousin: “We're good, let's start drafting.”

Accurate. Dense. Maria reads it twice.
Claude + MCP · reply 4521 SE Belmont: base zone R2.5; no overlay zones detected. ADU permitted under PCC 33.205.040: max 800 sf or 75% of main dwelling, whichever is less. Setbacks per 33.110.220: front 10', side 5', rear 5'. Height limit 20'. SDCs apply (water, sewer, transportation, parks). Stormwater management may trigger if impervious threshold exceeded.
PCC 33.205.040 PCC 33.110.220 PortlandMaps

Maria circles “SDC” and “impervious threshold.” Googles both. Sort of understands.

It reads like a person wrote it for her.
Claude + MCP + enrichment · reply Your property: 4521 SE Belmont, zoned R2.5. An ADU is allowed. A “tiny house on wheels” would not count as a permanent residence here — you want a tiny house on foundation.

Size & placement (PCC 33.205.040): up to 800 sf, max 20' tall. Setbacks (PCC 33.110.220): 10' front, 5' sides, 5' rear.

Heads up: the pear tree on PortlandMaps may be a regulated tree under Title 11 — get an Urban Forestry assessment before you site the slab.

Order: (1) free zoning confirmation at BDS, (2) Urban Forestry tree review, (3) BES sewer letter, (4) building permit + sub-permits, (5) erosion control.
PCC 33.205.040 PCC 33.110.220 Title 11 / UF BDS Code Guide

Maria has a checklist. She knows the tree is the long pole. Emails Urban Forestry first thing Monday.

Week 1 · Nov 19 Maria translates the answer into action
She designs to the wrong numbers.

Cousin Hector sketches a 28×32 footprint — 896 sq ft, comfortably “under 1,000.” Sited 5 feet off the rear fence. Maria orders a survey, books a foundation crew for January.

She knows the limit. Not the order of operations.

800 sq ft, 5-foot rear setback — correct. Hector redesigns to 768 sq ft. But Maria doesn't know whether to file building first or zoning first, doesn't know what BES stands for, and the AI used “impervious area” without defining it.

Three appointments booked.

Free zoning confirmation: scheduled for Nov 21. Urban Forestry site visit: Dec 1. Hector starts a 768 sq ft design with the slab pulled 13 ft from the pear's drip line. No surprises baked in.

Week 4 · Dec 10 The first real-world consequence shows up
Footings poured. No permit pulled.

The AI never said “file a zoning confirmation first.” Maria thought the building permit would come later. The crew breaks ground on a cold Tuesday. Concrete cures over the weekend.

Stuck in permit-portal limbo.

The BDS portal asks for a zoning confirmation number, then a BES sewer availability letter, then asks whether Urban Forestry has cleared the pear tree. Three different bureaus. She emails three different addresses. Two reply.

Tree cleared. Sewer letter in.

Pear tree: regulated, but the relocated slab clears the root protection zone — no mitigation triggered. BES confirms sewer capacity. Maria submits her building permit packet Dec 9 with all four supporting docs attached.

Month 3 · Feb 4, 2026 The system meets reality
A neighbor calls 311. Stop-work order.

A BDS inspector arrives Thursday at 11 AM and posts a yellow notice on the framing. The actual rules for 4521 SE Belmont:

What the code actually says Zone R2.5, ADU max 800 sq ft per PCC 33.205.040. Rear setback 5', but the slab also crowds the protected root zone of a 28" DBH pear — Title 11 Urban Forestry violation. Footings sit 96 sq ft over the size cap.

The structure has to come down. Or be cut. Either way, the slab is wrong.

Eight weeks of back-and-forth.

Zoning confirmation comes through Jan 14. Urban Forestry assessment: Feb 2 (the pear is regulated; mitigation plan required). BES sewer letter: still pending. Maria has paid for plans she can't yet submit. Hector has taken another job. The build is paused.

Permit issued. Ground broken.

Plan review came back with two minor corrections (smoke alarm spec, vapor barrier note). Hector resubmits in 4 days. Permit issued Jan 28. Footings poured Feb 3. Inspector signs off Feb 9.

Path A · out-of-box
−$16,700
Stop-work, demo, rebuild

The AI gave her a confident, wrong number. She acted on it. The city had to step in. The damage is the cost of pretending you have data you don't.

Path B · data-only
−8 wks
Right facts, wrong onboarding

Citations were perfect. Sequencing was opaque. Maria wasted two months figuring out which bureau to talk to in what order — data she didn't know to ask for.

Path C · data + a real question
✓
Permit by Jan, foundation by Feb

Same data, framed for a person without a code background. Acronyms defined, sequence given, the right warning surfaced (the tree). She acted — correctly — that night.

The hackathon brief asked: how might we help community members easily find and apply relevant rules to real-world situations?

For Maria, the answer is the difference between having her mom in the backyard by Memorial Day, or watching a $4,200 fine arrive in the mail and starting over in July.
